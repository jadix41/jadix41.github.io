Zone Rate Is the Mechanism

The edge framing numbers between Contreras and his backups are essentially the same across both seasons, so framing isn't driving the walk rate gap.

Brewers pitchers throw to the strike zone at a meaningfully higher rate when Contreras is behind the plate. A 6.2-point zone rate gap in 2024 and a 2.9-point gap in 2025 means pitchers are actively attacking hitters more with Contreras catching.

Whether that's pitch-calling, trust, or something harder to quantify, the behavioral change shows up consistently across different pitchers and different seasons.

Ever wonder how Contreras started 128 games behind the plate in 2025, a career high, while playing through a fractured left hand for most of the season? The Brewers knew what the data shows. Every game he missed meant a backup catching a staff that walks batters at a measurably higher rate without him. He caught as many games as he physically could because the organization understood the cost when he didn't.

From Sanchez to Jansen, and Back Again

Gary Sanchez caught 31 games in 2024 with 1,113 plate appearances behind him. The staff walked batters at an 8.3% rate in those games, compared to 7.4% with Contreras. Zone rate dropped 6.2 points.

The Brewers replaced him with Eric Haase to open 2025, then added Danny Jansen at the trade deadline in August. Jansen's reputation as one of the better defensive catchers in the league made him a logical target when the backup situation wasn't working.

Now Sanchez is back for 2026, and the signing is defensible. He hits left-handed pitching better than almost any backup catcher available, which addresses a real lineup gap. The Brewers aren't signing him to replace Contreras. They're signing him to fill the DH slot against southpaws and spot-catch when Contreras needs a day.

The question is whether the staff can absorb the receiving tradeoff on those days. This data suggests some pitchers handle it better than others.

The Wilson Problem, and Who Inherits It

Bryse Wilson is the clearest illustration of what catcher-sensitive command actually looks like. With Contreras in 2024, he posted a 5.6% walk rate and attacked the zone 55.7% of the time, functioning as a contact-oriented starter. Without Contreras, his zone rate fell nearly 10 points to 45.8% and his walk rate jumped to 8.9%. Same pitcher, radically different output depending on who was catching.

Wilson isn't on the 2026 roster, but his profile has clear successors. Chad Patrick posted a 7.1-point zone rate drop with backup catchers in 2025 and a 3.2% walk rate increase, the most Wilson-like numbers on the current staff. Aaron Ashby's zone rate dropped 7.0 points and his walk rate jumped 6.1 points without Contreras.

These aren't peripheral arms. Patrick made 23 starts as a rookie, logged 119.2 innings, and threw nine postseason frames with 11 strikeouts, including a starring role in the Game 5 win over the Cubs that sent Milwaukee to the NLCS.

Ashby made 43 appearances in 2025, averaged a career-high 97.5 mph on his sinker, and was trusted as the opener in both the NLDS and NLCS. The Brewers leaned on both of them all season and even harder when it mattered most.

They are core pieces, not depth options, and both show meaningful catcher sensitivity in the data.

2026 and the Sanchez Re-Sign

The Brewers re-signed Gary Sanchez as backup for 2026. His bat against left-handed pitching addresses a real lineup need, and the expectation is that Contreras catches the bulk of games regardless.

This data isn't an argument against the signing. It's a map of which days carry the most risk. Patrick, Ashby, and Priester are productive pitchers with Contreras behind the plate. Each has shown they walk more and attack the zone less without him.

On the days Sanchez catches, those are the starts most likely to get expensive early.
